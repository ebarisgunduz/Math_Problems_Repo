# Task 1 — Recognizing Counting Models

For each situation, identify the most appropriate combinatorial model:

| Problem | Appropriate model | Reason |
|---|---|---|
| Arranging 7 students in a line. | **permutation** | All 7 students are arranged, and order matters. |
| Choosing 4 members of a committee from 12 people. | **combination** | We only choose 4 people, and order does **not** matter. |
| Assigning gold, silver, and bronze medals among 15 athletes. | **k-permutation (ordered selection without repetition)** | We select 3 athletes, and the order matters because gold, silver, and bronze are different positions. |
| Forming a 6-digit PIN code. | **sequence with repetition** | Each of the 6 positions matters, and digits can repeat. |
| Arranging the letters of the word BANANA. | **permutation with repeated elements** | Letters are arranged, but some letters repeat: A appears 3 times, N appears 2 times. |
| Seating 6 people around a round table. | **circular permutation** | Arrangements are around a circle, so rotations are considered the same. |

## Final Answers
1. Arranging 7 students in a line → **permutation**  
2. Choosing 4 members of a committee from 12 people → **combination**  
3. Assigning gold, silver, and bronze medals among 15 athletes → **k-permutation**  
4. Forming a 6-digit PIN code → **sequence with repetition**  
5. Arranging the letters of the word BANANA → **permutation with repeated elements**  
6. Seating 6 people around a round table → **circular permutation**
